## The problem

In a field clinic without reliable mains power, a rural maternal-health post in Vietnam, or the first 72 hours after an earthquake, the constraint is not technique — it is *equipment*. A standard vacutainer system depends on a supply chain. When that supply chain breaks, the cost of drawing 4 mL of venous blood becomes infinite.

Lifeline is one answer to that constraint. It is a fully 3D-printable blood sampler, vacuum-actuated by a hand-pumped bellows, with a microneedle array, a check-valve manifold, and a sealed collection chamber.

> Sub-ten-dollar bill of materials. No electricity. Reproducible from a single SD card and a desktop FDM printer.

## Engineering decisions

The whole design pivots on three commitments.

**Vacuum from the operator's hand, not a bulb.** A silicone bellows compressed between two printed PETG plates produces ~25 kPa of negative pressure across three pumps. This is enough to fill a 4 mL chamber in 18–24 seconds at body temperature. A bulb design was 40% cheaper but unreliable across operators with smaller hand spans.

**Check-valve manifold over a single duckbill.** Two opposing one-way valves, each a flexure printed in-place, eliminate the failure mode where a single valve gets fouled by a clot and the entire sample is lost. The cost is one extra hour of print time per unit.

**Microneedle array, not a single 21G.** A 7×7 array of 800 µm needles distributes the puncture force, reduces patient flinching, and tolerates skin variation across paediatric to geriatric patients. The trade-off is shorter draw depth — which is acceptable for venous capture against a tourniquet, not arterial.

## The CAD release

All Fusion360 source files, OpenSCAD parametric variants, print profiles, sterilisation protocol, and a clinical-handling cheat-sheet are released under **CC-BY-4.0**. A field-ready PDF assembly guide is included so the design can be reproduced without internet access — only a printed sheet and the printer.

The licence choice was deliberate. CC-BY-4.0 lets a hospital, an NGO, or a university lab adapt the design without needing to negotiate with us. Attribution is the only ask.

## What's next

A clinical-grade variant with a moulded silicone gasket (replacing the printed flexure check valve) is in design review. Field testing with a partner clinic in central Vietnam is being scoped under SAIL.
