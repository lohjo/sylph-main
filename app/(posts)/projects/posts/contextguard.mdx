## The thesis

Most fact-checking happens *after* a reader has formed a belief. ContextGuard is built on the opposite premise: the moment of friction is the moment a claim is being read. Surface the context there, in the article itself, and the reader does not need to decide to verify — they verify by default.

## What it does

When a reader lands on a news article, ContextGuard performs three operations in the page:

1. **Source profile.** Resolves the publisher, ownership, ideological positioning (where known), correction history, and whether the source has retracted similar claims. Renders as a small inline card next to the byline.
2. **Claim extraction.** Highlights factual claims at sentence granularity. Each highlight is hoverable.
3. **Contradicting evidence.** For each claim, surfaces the strongest contradicting article from a curated index, filtered by recency and source diversity.

The UX rule is *one badge, one decision*. No dashboards, no sidebar that demands attention. The reader either ignores the inline card and reads on, or hovers and sees more.

## Decisions for non-technical readers

Building for non-technical readers means refusing to show your work.

- No confidence percentages. They imply false precision and feel adversarial.
- No "AI-generated" labels for opinion pieces. Genre confusion is a different problem from misinformation.
- No partisan framing. The extension says *"this claim is contradicted by"* and never *"this is wrong."* The user makes the call.

## Stack

A Next.js + TypeScript codebase (shared between the extension popup and a small admin web), a content-script bundle that ships under 60 KB gzipped, and a server-side claim-resolution API that uses a vector index of curated outlets.

The hardest engineering trade-off was deciding *not* to use an LLM at request time. Latency was unacceptable and hallucination risk was a non-starter for a tool that asks readers to trust it. The current pipeline uses an LLM offline to enrich the index, and serves precomputed contradictions at runtime.
